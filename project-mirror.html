Mirror

A Responsive E-Commerce Website

Overview

The Problem

Although Mirror is a successful brick and mortar chain of stores, they need a fresh new look and a user-friendly website to compete in a market where other brands are well established. Mirror has no online presence and want to start digitising their store in order to increase sales, reach new customers and keep up with the times. Mirror has also acknowledged the fact that they have plenty of remaining inventory in their warehouses that are very difficult to move if there are only a few pieces left, for which online selling would be great.

Research

Research Goals

  • To study and discover the differences in potential/existing customers’ common shopping behaviors, habits, motivation, and preferences when shopping online and in stores. Research current market trends of online clothing stores and what is currently working for other e-commerce companies as well as competitors
  • Learn about a user’s pain points while purchasing clothes online
  • Understand why users shop for clothes online versus in-store and what triggers a user to shop online
  • Analyze essential features of the store’s website to include in the website redesign



I began my research by looking at general information about the e-commerce industry. Next, I took note of common trends of online clothing store websites. This secondary data provided perspective on the subject matter and the potential needs of current online shoppers. Since there are many clothing stores, I paired the available research with a competitive analysis to understand how Mirror compares against other brands. I evaluated the strengths and weaknesses of websites that belonged to direct and indirect competitors. The comparative model allowed me to understand better customer expectations and how Mirror can position itself to stand out among other competing companies.

Secondary Research Findings

  • 71% of shoppers believe they will get a better deal online than in stores
  • Still, 49% prefer physical stores
  • 15% have made purchases by following a link from social media sites
  • 28% of U.S. adults purchase a few times a month online
  • 53% say they always or almost always read online reviews when buying something for the first time

Competitive Analysis

I compared direct competitors shown in the right picture, as well as rental wardrobes and used clothing stores. I highlighted their strengths and weaknesses and identified their provisional personas. This was done in order to make more informed decisions about Mirror’s product strategy.

User Interview & Survey

To gain primary research from shoppers, I conducted contextual inquiry interviews with 3 participants about their shopping methods and collected data from 15 survey responses. The interviews consisted of open-ended questions regarding in-store and online shopping experiences, while the survey consisted of various multiple-choice questions, short answers, and scaled response questions. Ultimately, I learned more about how shoppers behave and see patterns in shopping needs.

Most common needs & desires:

  • Customer reviews and pictures are a priority when it comes to online shopping.
  • Secure and reliable alternative payment methods (Paypal, Apple Pay, Google Pay and Afterpay)
  • All participants agreed that easy navigation and filters are must
  • Transparent return policies

The main Pain Points that have resulted from the research included:

  • Website or app is not easy to navigate
  • Poor material quality and unclear product information
  • Get disappointed in the fitting, they bought it because it fits the model, but it appears it doesn't fit their body type
  • Lack of product reviews and real photos
  • Poor search and filter options
  • Users often buy the item but don’t wear it, because they unsure how to combine it with other items

Define

For the product to be fully successful it's important to have a balance between business and users' goals. I facilitated this by clearly identifying both business and users' goals, and where the goals overlapped I formed the mutual goal to help inform our design. I created this project goals table to help us provide a reference and an overview of how the balance and alignment of the goals can help us create a website and branding that reflects the needs of both the business and the users.

The responsive website design will:

  • Show good quality pictures as well as video of the product
  • Allow users to filter products by color, size, type, and body fit
  • Provide a more personalized user experience by adding the "ask fashion expert" function
  • Show real photo prom the customers
  • Allow finding the needed size of user
  • Show review of the product
  • Show the detailed description for items

Empathy Map

I constructed an empathy map by organizing the data points from a consensus from the interview participants and survey responses. Then, I added this to my persona to add a realistic character. With this information, I could gather the user's needs and set priorities for product needs.

Card Sorting

Now that I have a better understanding of the project goals, I move on to conducting a remote Open-Card Sort exercise to help me get user feedback and observe how users naturally organize various clothing items.

In this study, I discovered that the most common trend was to categorize the cards by the type of item. With this information, I can consider the information architecture of Mirror's website to be intuitive for the users.

Wireframes & Visual Design

Before creating digital wireframes, I did a brainstorming session to sketch some wireframe ideas for Mirror’s homepage quickly. By doing this, I could sketch out different layout ideas for displaying the content and invite feedback early on from peers to help me find the gaps. Using Sketch, I created mid-fidelity wireframes. While they contain a little more detail than required, these wireframes helped me develop the UI design's basic layout and visual hierarchy before adding colors and images.

User Testing

Five participants were recruited to test the prototype. Each participant was given the same hypothetical scenario and task to complete. Tests were conducted: 3 person via video call with screen sharing and 2 in-person.

The task included:

  • Find a dress
  • Find more information about the material
  • Put a dress in size M into the cart

Test objectives:

  • Track and observe how users navigate the site to complete tasks
  • Observe if user check out the “complete your outfit” section
  • Identify areas of confusion or error for the user

Affinity Map

I organized my test results into an affinity map to visually see where users where experiencing similarities.

Final Thoughts & Next Steps

My main takeaway here was without doubt – listening more and try to ask the right questions.

Throughout this process, I had preconceived ideas about how a clothing retailer's site should look, how people should interact, and the features it should have. For example, I was surprised that many people use the "search" icon, not the menu, to find the needed category. This situation showed me that my assumptions are just assumptions and helped me get out of my head and empathize more.

The next steps for the project would be to continue wireframing based on received test results, prototyping more features, and conducting usability testing for the remaining pages of the website. It is essential to constantly update the website to meet user needs and coincide with industry standards.

Long-term:

  • Test responsive website
  • Do further research on the “ Ask Fashion Expert” function
